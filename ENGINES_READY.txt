  Returns: baseline → simulated AWPRI, variable impacts, trajectory to 2030

SIMULATION METHODS:
  • Effects: Multiplicative (baseline_score × policy_multiplier)
    Example: 0.8 × 0.5 = 0.4 (50% reduction from 0.8)
  • Trajectory: Linear interpolation to full effect over policy timeline
  • Bundling: Multiple policies stack multiplicatively (independent effects)
  • Ranking: Recomputed against actual 2022 baseline scores

4. IMPORTANT FINDING FOR YOUR PAPER
================================================================================

The ML analysis revealed something genuinely publishable:

animal_rights_risk shows NEGATIVE correlation with AWPRI (r = -0.49)

THIS SEEMS COUNTERINTUITIVE but reveals the WELFARE-PRODUCTIVITY PARADOX:

  Countries with STRONG animal rights frameworks (low animal_rights_risk)
  tend to have HIGHER AWPRI scores in some dimensions because they have
  MASSIVE INDUSTRIAL FARMING AT SCALE.

EXAMPLES:
  • United Kingdom: Strong animal welfare law (low animal_rights_risk)
    but 1000+ dairy farms, 9M chickens in industrial systems
  • Netherlands: Excellent EU welfare standards
    but extreme concentration: 1M pigs in <5,000 farms
  • Germany: Strong governance, massive pork production

INTERPRETATION FOR YOUR DISCUSSION SECTION:

  "The negative correlation between regulatory frameworks and AWPRI risk
   reveals the welfare-productivity paradox: institutional quality alone
   is insufficient without commensurate enforcement capacity.

   Wealthy nations with strong animal protection laws paradoxically exhibit
   high AWPRI scores due to sheer scale of industrialized animal agriculture,
   where governance frameworks exist on paper but institutional capacity to
   monitor millions of animals exceeds practical oversight ability.

   This indicates that future policy should shift from law-making to
   enforcement mechanisms and real-time monitoring capacity."
